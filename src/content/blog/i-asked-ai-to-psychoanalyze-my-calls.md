I run a lot of calls. Partner calls, 1:1s, sales demos, board meetings, geek-out sessions with friends. On a normal week it's somewhere between twenty and thirty conversations, all recorded, all transcribed, all flowing into my Context Engine.

A couple of weeks ago I did something with all of that data that I haven't been able to stop thinking about since.

I gave AI one prompt:

> **"Look at all my calls and interactions through the lens of a diagnostic psychiatrist and identify patterns."**

What came back stopped me cold. Not because it was clever. Because it was *true*. Patterns in how I avoid certain conversations. How I open far more loops than I close. How my energy shifts depending on who I'm talking to and which business we're talking about. Things my wife could have told me. Things a good coach would have gotten to after months of sessions. Surfaced in about a minute, from my own words, with receipts.

I want to walk you through where this idea came from, what happened when I took the results into an actual session with a coach, and the one ingredient that makes the whole thing work — because without it, this prompt gives you nothing.

## Credit where it's due: this was Jock's idea

The spark for this came from my friend [Jock Gordon](https://jockgordon.com). Jock runs the Attachment Project, and he thinks about human psychology at a scale most therapists never will: over ten million survey responses on attachment, millions more on emotional regulation and self-esteem, hundreds of thousands of qualitative text answers. When Jock talks about patterns in human behavior, he isn't speculating. He's read the data.

Talking with him, it clicked: I'm sitting on my own behavioral dataset. Every call I take is me, unfiltered, in real situations — negotiating, avoiding, deflecting, getting excited, getting defensive. That's not a calendar archive. That's raw psychological data. Jock's world made me see mine differently, and the psychiatrist prompt came straight out of that conversation. Thank you, Jock.

## What the AI actually found

I won't share the full report — some of it is genuinely private — but I'll share the finding that hit hardest, because I suspect it applies to more entrepreneurs than just me:

**I open loops compulsively and close them reluctantly.**

Across two weeks of calls, the AI counted the commitments I made — "I'll send you that," "let me intro you to," "I'll review the draft" — against the ones I closed. The ratio was not flattering. It also noticed *when* I open loops: mostly in the last ten minutes of a call, mostly when I'm excited about the person, mostly as a way of extending the relationship rather than because the commitment itself matters.

Seeing that in black and white changed my behavior faster than any productivity system I've ever tried. I now have a daily check-in prompt built on top of it. Every morning it asks me two questions:

> **How many loops did you open yesterday? How many did you close?**

That's it. No dashboard, no app, no streak counter. Just a mirror, held up daily, by a system that was actually listening yesterday.

## Then Nikolai took it somewhere deeper

Here's where it got really interesting.

I shared the experiment with [Nikolai Ladanyi](https://scaleup.de) — Scaling Up coach, author, and one of the most thoughtful people I know on the intersection of business and inner work. Nikolai loved the idea and immediately improved it. His reframe:

**Don't ask for a diagnostic psychiatrist. Ask for an IFS therapist.**

His reasoning was sharp: the psychiatrist lens pathologizes. It looks for what's *wrong* with you, and the output reads like a diagnosis. The Internal Family Systems lens — after Dr. Richard Schwartz — does something different. It assumes every behavior, even the self-sabotaging ones, is a *part* of you trying to protect you. All parts are welcome. Same data, completely different relationship with what you find.

Then he didn't just theorize. He ran a session with me.

We took one of the patterns the AI had surfaced — a "distractor" part that pulls me to the couch and the scroll instead of the work — and Nikolai guided me through actually talking to it. Not fighting it. Understanding what it was protecting me from. We traced it back to my early teens, to the years right after my father died, when avoiding things was the only protection available to a kid who was told, in so many words, to not feel the pain.

By the end of the session, that part had a new name and a new job: instead of protecting me through avoidance, its job is to speak up — to say no out loud when I'm overcommitting, instead of saying it silently through wasted evenings. The distractor, it turns out, was never lazy. It was a regulator for a guy who says yes too much. It just had a terrible communication style.

I've done a lot of personal development work over the years. This session was one of the most effective hours I've spent, and it started from a pattern an AI found in my call transcripts. Nikolai is now writing about exactly this intersection — how AI changes personal development itself — and his core thesis deserves its own paragraph:

**AI is removing the triggers that used to force founders to grow.** Complexity — big teams, hard conversations, cross-cultural friction — used to push leaders into self-reflection whether they liked it or not. AI increasingly absorbs that complexity. Which means the growth that used to happen *to* you now has to be chosen *by* you. Personal development is shifting from extrinsic to intrinsic, and the people who don't choose it will end up running companies they no longer understand. When his book comes out, read it.

## None of this works without the memory

Now the part I really want you to get, because it's the difference between this being a party trick and a practice.

If you paste one meeting transcript into ChatGPT and ask it to psychoanalyze you, you'll get horoscope material. Plausible, generic, gone by tomorrow. The model isn't the magic. **The context is the magic.**

The reason my one-line prompt produced something a coach would need months to see is that it wasn't looking at one call. It was looking at *me* — through my [Context Engine](https://contextengine.com), which holds:

- **Every call and meeting** I've had, transcribed and cross-linked
- **My email and calendar** — what I commit to and what I actually do
- **My journals** — daily rolled into weekly, weekly into monthly
- **My health data** — WHOOP recovery, HRV, sleep, even blood work, so it can see *which meetings stress my body*, not just my calendar
- **My goals, values, and personality profiles** — so it knows what I say I want, and can compare it with how I actually behave

That last point is the whole game. Self-deception lives in the gap between what we say we value and what we repeatedly do. No single conversation exposes that gap. A permanent memory of hundreds of them can't help but expose it.

I [wrote recently](/blog/the-foundational-building-block-of-an-ai-native-company) that the first thing to build in an AI-native company isn't AI, it's memory. This is the same principle turned inward. The first thing to build for AI-powered self-development isn't a better prompt. It's a memory that actually knows you. Because it runs on my own server, with my own keys, I'm willing to feed it things I would never type into someone else's chatbot — and that willingness is exactly what makes the mirror accurate.

## Try it yourself

You don't need my exact setup to start. Here's the ladder:

1. **Minimum version:** export your last two weeks of meeting transcripts (Granola, Fireflies, whatever you use), drop them into Claude, and run the prompt. Even this will surprise you.
2. **Better:** use Nikolai's reframe. *"Look at all my calls and interactions through the lens of an IFS therapist, after Dr. Richard Schwartz. Identify my parts, what each one is protecting me from, and the patterns they create."*
3. **Best:** give the AI a permanent memory — calls, journals, health data, goals — so the mirror gets sharper every week instead of resetting every chat. That's what the Context Engine is for, and it's why I built my whole life on top of it.

And whatever the mirror shows you: take it to a human. The AI found my pattern. It took Jock's lens to know the data was there, and Nikolai's skill to turn the pattern into an actual shift. The machines are getting very good at showing us ourselves. What we do with what we see — that's still gloriously, stubbornly human work.
